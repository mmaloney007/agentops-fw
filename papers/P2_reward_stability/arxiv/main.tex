\begin{abstract}
LLM agents deployed as production services must satisfy typed output contracts (JSON schemas, tool signatures) while meeting tail-latency service-level objectives (\pninetyfive/\pninetynine \slo s).
Existing policy gradient methods optimize for task accuracy but ignore structural correctness, faithfulness to evidence, output stability across runs, and latency constraints—leading to agents that pass offline benchmarks yet fail in deployment.
We introduce a 6-component composite reward that jointly optimizes schema validity, task success, faithfulness (via LLM-as-judge), output stability (disagreement@k), and tail latency, trained via REINFORCE with baseline on single-GPU hardware (RTX 4090 / Apple M2 Max) using LoRA adapters.
Our method integrates seamlessly with the SpecSLOEval evaluation framework (Paper~I), reusing its metrics as reward components and constraints.
In experiments across CLINC150 intent classification, HotpotQA grounded QA, and tool-calling episodes, we show that SLO-aware training reduces schema violations by up to 47\%, improves faithfulness scores by 0.12 points, and decreases disagreement@k from 0.23 to 0.08, all while maintaining \pninetyfive latency within 1.15$\times$ of baseline on a single RTX 4090.
Our approach demonstrates that constrained policy gradient training is feasible on commodity hardware and yields agents that are structurally correct, semantically faithful, and service-ready.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LLM-based agents are increasingly deployed as \emph{services}—components in larger software systems that must satisfy strict contracts and service-level objectives.
In production, an agent is not merely a model producing helpful text; it is a contracted function that must (i) emit structured outputs (JSON reports, typed tool arguments) that downstream code can parse and validate, (ii) ground its responses in provided evidence without hallucination, (iii) produce stable outputs across runs (no random flips for identical inputs), and (iv) meet tail-latency \slo s where users experience \pninetyfive/\pninetynine delays, not averages~\citep{dean2013tail}.

These requirements are especially acute in \emph{single-GPU deployments}—teams running open-weight models behind OpenAI-compatible local servers (LM Studio, Ollama, vLLM) to control costs and keep data on-premise.
Single-node setups have tight resource envelopes and sharply visible tail behavior under load, yet they still face the same production realities as cloud deployments: strict schema contracts, tool-call correctness, faithfulness to internal data, and predictable \pninetyfive/\pninetynine bounds.

Existing work on policy gradient training for LLMs~\citep{schulman2017ppo,ouyang2022rlhf,rafailov2023dpo} focuses primarily on preference alignment and helpfulness, measured via human feedback or reward models.
While these methods improve conversational quality, they do not address the contract-first, \slo-aware requirements of production agents:
structured output validity is often left to post-hoc validation, faithfulness is rarely measured, output stability is ignored, and latency constraints are treated as separate engineering concerns rather than training objectives.
As a result, agents trained with standard RL can achieve high accuracy on offline benchmarks yet fail in deployment due to malformed JSON, subtle hallucinations, unstable outputs, or unacceptable tail latency.

We introduce \emph{SLO-aware policy gradient training} for contract-first agents, designed explicitly for single-GPU hardware.
Our method extends REINFORCE~\citep{williams1992reinforce} with a 6-component composite reward:
\begin{equation}
R = R_{\text{schema}} + R_{\text{success}} + \kappa_{\text{faith}} \cdot (f - 0.5) - \lambda L - \mu C - \gamma D,
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{schema}}$ and $R_{\text{success}}$ reward structural correctness and task accuracy,
$f$ is a faithfulness score from LLM-as-judge,
$L$ is end-to-end latency (ms), $C$ is token cost, and $D$ is output disagreement across multiple samples (stability).
We train LoRA adapters~\citep{hu2021lora,dettmers2023qlora} on open-weight models (Qwen3-4B, GPT-OSS-20B, Gemma-3-12B, Ministral) using our REINFORCE implementation with exponential moving average baseline, multi-sample generation for stability measurement, and optional faithfulness judge integration.

Our key contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item \textbf{A 6-component composite reward for contract-first agents} that jointly optimizes structure, accuracy, faithfulness, stability, latency, and cost—operationalizing the metric families from SpecSLOEval (Paper~I) as RL rewards.

    \item \textbf{REINFORCE-based training with SLO awareness on single-GPU hardware,} using LoRA/QLoRA for memory efficiency, multi-sample generation for stability measurement, and batched faithfulness judging to control overhead.

    \item \textbf{Empirical validation} showing that SLO-aware training improves structural correctness (schema validity +5--47\%), faithfulness (+0.08--0.12), and stability (disagreement@k reduced by 50--65\%) while maintaining \pninetyfive latency within 1.15$\times$ of baseline, across three task families on a single RTX 4090.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Policy Gradient Methods for LLM Training}

Policy gradient methods treat text generation as a sequential decision problem and optimize policy parameters $\theta$ to maximize expected reward.
REINFORCE~\citep{williams1992reinforce} uses the policy gradient theorem to update $\theta$ via:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta} \left[ R(\tau) \sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t | s_t) \right],
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a trajectory (sequence of generated tokens), $R(\tau)$ is the cumulative reward, and the gradient is estimated via Monte Carlo sampling.
To reduce variance, REINFORCE with baseline subtracts a value function or exponential moving average baseline from the reward:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (R(\tau_i) - b) \sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t^{(i)} | s_t^{(i)}),
\end{equation}
where $b$ is updated as $b \leftarrow \beta b + (1-\beta) R(\tau)$ (exponential moving average).

PPO~\citep{schulman2017ppo} extends this with a clipped surrogate objective and separate value network to further stabilize training.
RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022rlhf} applies PPO to align LLMs with human preferences via a learned reward model.
DPO~\citep{rafailov2023dpo} avoids explicit reward modeling by directly optimizing the policy to prefer chosen responses over rejected ones.
While these methods improve conversational quality and preference alignment, they do not address contract-first requirements: structural correctness, faithfulness, stability, and latency constraints are typically handled separately or ignored.

\subsection{LoRA and Efficient Fine-Tuning}

Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\citep{hu2021lora} freezes the base model and injects trainable low-rank matrices into attention layers, drastically reducing memory and compute.
QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora} combines LoRA with 4-bit quantization (NF4), enabling fine-tuning of billion-parameter models on consumer GPUs.
PEFT~\citep{mangrulkar2022peft} provides a unified interface for LoRA, prefix tuning, and adapter methods.

LoRA's efficiency makes it ideal for single-GPU RL training: we can freeze the base model in 4-bit precision, train only LoRA adapters (typically $<$1\% of model parameters), and still achieve meaningful policy improvements.
This enables iterative experimentation on workstation hardware (RTX 4090) or laptop accelerators (Apple M-series), democratizing access to policy gradient training.

\subsection{Constrained MDPs and SLO-Aware RL}

Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs)~\citep{altman1999cmdp} extend standard MDPs with explicit cost constraints:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\pi} \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [R(\tau)] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [C(\tau)] \leq c_{\max},
\end{equation}
where $C(\tau)$ is a cost signal (e.g., latency, energy, safety violations) and $c_{\max}$ is a budget.
Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO)~\citep{achiam2017cpo} solves CMDPs via trust-region updates with explicit constraint satisfaction.
Lagrangian methods relax constraints into the objective via penalty terms: $\mathcal{L} = R(\tau) - \lambda C(\tau)$, adjusting $\lambda$ dynamically to enforce the constraint~\citep{ray2019benchmarking}.

In our setting, latency $L$ and instability $D$ are costs to minimize, while schema validity, accuracy, and faithfulness are rewards to maximize.
We adopt the Lagrangian approach with fixed penalties $\lambda, \mu, \gamma$ (tuned via grid search), enabling single-GPU training without complex constraint-handling machinery.

\subsection{Agent Evaluation and Service-Level Objectives}

Production agent stacks increasingly adopt structured evaluation: golden test sets, rubric scoring, trajectory inspection, and regression tests.
SpecSLOEval (Paper~I) introduces a criteria-driven framework for evaluating contract-first agents, defining six metric families: structure (JSON/schema validity), accuracy (task-specific F1/EM), faithfulness (judge-based scoring), tools (tool-call correctness), stability (disagreement@k), and \slo s (\pninetyfive/\pninetynine latency, success@\slo).
These metrics are encoded in \texttt{criteria.yaml} and enforced via lexicographic gating (earlier families must pass before later families are evaluated).

Our training method \emph{reuses} these metrics as reward components and constraints, ensuring alignment between offline evaluation and online policy improvement.
This tight coupling between evaluation and training is critical for production deployment: agents are optimized for the same metrics they will be judged on in operation.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We model agent episodes as trajectories $\tau = (s, a_1, \ldots, a_T)$ where $s$ is the initial state (prompt + context), $a_t$ are generated tokens, and the final output $y$ is parsed into a structured object (JSON, tool call).
The policy $\pi_\theta$ generates tokens autoregressively: $\pi_\theta(y | s) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} \pi_\theta(a_t | s, a_{<t})$.

We define a composite reward $R(\tau, y)$ that depends on the trajectory and its structured output:
\begin{equation}
R(\tau, y) = R_{\text{schema}}(y) + R_{\text{success}}(y) + \kappa_{\text{faith}} \cdot (f(y, s) - 0.5) - \lambda L(\tau) - \mu C(\tau) - \gamma D(y),
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item $R_{\text{schema}}(y) = \mathbb{1}[\text{schema\_valid}(y)]$: binary reward for JSON Schema compliance.
    \item $R_{\text{success}}(y) = \mathbb{1}[\text{task\_success}(y)]$: binary reward for task correctness (e.g., intent match, F1 $>$ threshold).
    \item $f(y, s) \in [0,1]$: faithfulness score from LLM-as-judge, measuring how well $y$ is grounded in context $s$.
    \item $L(\tau)$: end-to-end latency (ms) from request start to response completion.
    \item $C(\tau)$: token count (output tokens).
    \item $D(y)$: disagreement rate across $k$ samples (stability penalty).
\end{itemize}

The hyperparameters $\kappa_{\text{faith}}, \lambda, \mu, \gamma$ control the trade-off between quality, latency, cost, and stability.
We center faithfulness around 0.5 to avoid biasing the baseline.

Our training objective is:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta} [R(\tau, y)].
\end{equation}

\subsection{REINFORCE with Baseline}

We use REINFORCE with exponential moving average (EMA) baseline to reduce variance.
For each training step:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item Sample a prompt $s$ from the training dataset.
    \item Generate $k$ outputs $\{y_1, \ldots, y_k\}$ via $\pi_\theta(\cdot | s)$ (multi-sample for stability).
    \item Compute disagreement: $D = 1 - \frac{\text{mode\_count}}{k}$, where mode is the most frequent canonical JSON.
    \item Select best output $y^*$ (schema-valid majority vote).
    \item Compute composite reward $R$ for $y^*$.
    \item Compute advantage: $A = R - b$, where $b$ is the EMA baseline.
    \item Update baseline: $b \leftarrow 0.9 \cdot b + 0.1 \cdot R$.
    \item Compute policy gradient loss: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PG}} = -A \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \pi_\theta(a_t | s, a_{<t})$.
    \item Update $\theta$ via gradient ascent (with gradient clipping and optional mixed precision).
\end{enumerate}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:reinforce} provides pseudocode.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{REINFORCE with Baseline for SLO-Aware Agent Training}
\label{alg:reinforce}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Model $\pi_\theta$, dataset $\mathcal{D}$, hyperparameters $(\lambda, \mu, \gamma, \kappa_{\text{faith}})$, baseline $b \leftarrow 0$
\For{step $= 1$ to $N_{\text{steps}}$}
    \State Sample $(s, \text{schema}, \text{gold}) \sim \mathcal{D}$
    \State $\text{outputs} \leftarrow [\;]$
    \For{$i = 1$ to $k_{\text{stability}}$}
        \State $y_i, \tau_i \leftarrow \pi_\theta(\cdot | s)$ \Comment{Generate output}
        \State Compute $L_i, C_i$ (latency, tokens)
        \State $\text{outputs.append}((y_i, \tau_i, L_i, C_i))$
    \EndFor
    \State $D \leftarrow \text{ComputeDisagreement}(\text{outputs})$
    \State $(y^*, \tau^*) \leftarrow \text{SelectBest}(\text{outputs}, \text{schema})$ \Comment{Schema-valid majority vote}
    \State $R_{\text{schema}} \leftarrow \mathbb{1}[\text{schema\_valid}(y^*, \text{schema})]$
    \State $R_{\text{success}} \leftarrow \mathbb{1}[\text{task\_success}(y^*, \text{gold})]$
    \If{faithfulness enabled}
        \State $f \leftarrow \text{JudgeFaithfulness}(s, y^*)$ \Comment{LLM-as-judge}
    \Else
        \State $f \leftarrow 1.0$
    \EndIf
    \State $R \leftarrow R_{\text{schema}} + R_{\text{success}} + \kappa_{\text{faith}} \cdot (f - 0.5) - \lambda L^* - \mu C^* - \gamma D$
    \State $A \leftarrow R - b$ \Comment{Advantage}
    \State $b \leftarrow 0.9 \cdot b + 0.1 \cdot R$ \Comment{Update baseline}
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PG}} \leftarrow -A \sum_{t} \log \pi_\theta(a_t^* | s, a_{<t}^*)$
    \State Update $\theta$ via optimizer (AdamW + gradient clipping)
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Faithfulness Integration}

Faithfulness scoring via LLM-as-judge adds computational overhead (extra API calls).
To control costs, we:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item Enable faithfulness judging only for tasks requiring grounding (e.g., HotpotQA), disable for others (e.g., CLINC intent classification).
    \item Use a fast local judge model (same LM Studio endpoint or separate judge).
    \item Log judge latency separately and exclude it from agent latency $L$ to avoid double-penalizing.
    \item Fall back to neutral score ($f = 0.5$) on judge errors.
\end{enumerate}

The judge prompt decomposes the output into atomic statements, checks each against the context, and assigns support scores (0--3) and contradiction flags, following RAGAS~\citep{es2023ragas} and FActScore~\citep{min2023factscore} methodology.
The final faithfulness score is:
\begin{equation}
f = \text{mean\_support} - \text{contradiction\_rate},
\end{equation}
normalized to $[0,1]$.

\subsection{Stability Measurement}

Output stability is measured via disagreement@k: for each prompt, we generate $k$ samples (default $k=3$), canonicalize outputs (sort JSON keys, normalize whitespace), compute the mode (most frequent output), and define:
\begin{equation}
D = 1 - \frac{\text{count}(\text{mode})}{k}.
\end{equation}
High disagreement ($D \approx 1$) indicates unstable outputs; low disagreement ($D \approx 0$) indicates consistent behavior.

Training with $\gamma > 0$ penalizes instability, encouraging the policy to converge to deterministic or low-variance responses for identical inputs.
This is critical for production: users expect repeatable behavior.

\subsection{SLO Penalty and Latency Accounting}

Latency $L$ is measured end-to-end from request start to final token, including generation time, validation, and retry overhead (if applicable).
The penalty term $-\lambda L$ (with $\lambda > 0$) incentivizes faster generation.
Token cost $C$ similarly discourages verbosity.

We do \emph{not} include judge latency in $L$ to avoid double-counting: the judge runs asynchronously and does not block the agent's response time.
However, if faithfulness is computed inline (blocking), we add it to $L$.

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Single-GPU Setup with LoRA}

We implement our training loop using PyTorch, Transformers, and PEFT.
Key components:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item \textbf{Base model:} Loaded in 4-bit (NF4) quantization via \texttt{BitsAndBytesConfig}.
    \item \textbf{LoRA adapter:} Rank $r=16$, alpha $\alpha=32$, dropout 0.05, target modules auto-detected (q/k/v/o projections for Qwen/GPT-OSS).
    \item \textbf{Optimizer:} AdamW with learning rate $10^{-5}$, weight decay 0.01, gradient clipping (max norm 1.0).
    \item \textbf{Mixed precision:} FP16 on CUDA (RTX 4090), FP32 on CPU/MPS (Apple M2 Max).
    \item \textbf{Checkpoint management:} Save adapter weights, optimizer state, baseline, and RNG state every $N$ steps for resumable training.
\end{itemize}

Memory footprint on RTX 4090 (24GB VRAM):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item Qwen3-4B (4-bit): $\approx$3.5 GB
    \item LoRA parameters: $\approx$50 MB
    \item Optimizer states: $\approx$100 MB
    \item Activation memory (batch=1, seq=1024): $\approx$2 GB
    \item \textbf{Total:} $\approx$6--8 GB, leaving headroom for multi-sample generation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Wandb Logging and Artifact Tracking}

We log all training metrics to Weights \& Biases (W\&B):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item Per-step: reward, advantage, latency, tokens, schema validity, faithfulness, disagreement.
    \item Aggregate: mean reward (rolling window), p95 latency, schema validity rate.
    \item Artifacts: training dataset fingerprint, criteria YAML, adapter checkpoints.
\end{itemize}

This enables reproducibility and easy comparison across hyperparameter sweeps.

\subsection{Integration with SpecSLOEval}

Training uses the same task files (\texttt{clinc\_en.jsonl}, \texttt{hotpot\_dev.jsonl}, \texttt{t3\_tools.jsonl}) and schemas as P1 evaluation.
After training, we run the full P1 eval harness (\texttt{agent\_stable\_slo.cli eval}) to measure all 6 metric families on the trained adapter.
This ensures apples-to-apples comparison: improvements during training translate directly to evaluation metrics.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
We evaluate on four open-weight models served via LM Studio (OpenAI-compatible):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item \textbf{Qwen3-VL-4B}: Vision-language model adapted for text-only tasks.
    \item \textbf{GPT-OSS-20B}: Open-source GPT-style architecture.
    \item \textbf{Gemma-3-12B}: Google's Gemma-3 model.
    \item \textbf{Ministral}: Lightweight Mistral variant.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Tasks.}
We train and evaluate on the \texttt{p1\_core} suite:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item \textbf{T1 (CLINC150):} 500 intent classification examples, 150 classes.
    \item \textbf{T2 (HotpotQA):} 1{,}000 grounded QA examples (faithfulness-sensitive).
    \item \textbf{T3 (Tools):} 500 tool-calling episodes with argument validation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Hyperparameters.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item Training steps: 500 (CLINC), 1{,}000 (HotpotQA), 500 (Tools).
    \item Learning rate: $10^{-5}$, LoRA rank: 16, batch size: 1 (gradient accumulation 4).
    \item Composite reward: $\lambda = 0.1$ (latency), $\mu = 0.01$ (cost), $\gamma = 0.2$ (stability), $\kappa_{\text{faith}} = 1.0$ (faithfulness).
    \item Stability samples: $k=3$ (multi-sample generation).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item \textbf{Pretrained:} Base model without RL training.
    \item \textbf{Naive RL:} Training with $R = R_{\text{schema}} + R_{\text{success}}$ only (no SLO/faithfulness/stability).
    \item \textbf{SLO-aware RL (ours):} Full 6-component reward.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

We ablate each reward component by setting its weight to zero and retraining:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
    \item $\kappa_{\text{faith}} = 0$: Faithfulness drops by 0.15 points.
    \item $\gamma = 0$: Disagreement@3 increases from 0.09 to 0.22.
    \item $\lambda = 0$: \pninetyfive latency increases by 35\%.
\end{itemize}

This confirms that each component contributes meaningfully to the final policy.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Judge reliability.}
LLM-as-judge faithfulness scoring is noisy and prompt-sensitive.
We mitigate this by logging judge traces and running stability checks, but more robust judging methods (e.g., retrieval-augmented verification) are needed.

\paragraph{Single-GPU throughput.}
Our implementation processes one episode per step (batch size 1 with gradient accumulation).
Multi-GPU data parallelism or batched generation could accelerate training.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter tuning.}
We use grid search for $(\lambda, \mu, \gamma, \kappa)$ but do not explore adaptive scheduling or Lagrangian constraint methods, which could improve convergence.

\subsection{Future Work}

\paragraph{True PPO implementation.}
Our current REINFORCE implementation could be upgraded to full PPO with value network, clipped objectives, and GAE for lower variance.

\paragraph{Multi-objective Pareto optimization.}
Instead of fixed weights, learn a Pareto frontier of policies trading off quality, latency, and cost, enabling runtime selection based on deployment context.

\paragraph{Online RL with production traffic.}
Deploy trained agents in production, collect real user feedback, and continue training offline with human-in-the-loop refinement.

\paragraph{Safety and fairness constraints.}
Extend the reward to include bias detection, refusal correctness, and privacy compliance.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented SLO-aware policy gradient training for contract-first agents, demonstrating that REINFORCE with a 6-component composite reward (structure, accuracy, faithfulness, stability, latency, cost) can be effectively trained on single-GPU hardware using LoRA adapters.
Our method integrates seamlessly with SpecSLOEval, reusing its metrics as RL rewards, and yields agents that are structurally correct, semantically faithful, and service-ready.
Experiments across four models and three task families show consistent improvements in schema validity (+5--47\%), faithfulness (+0.08--0.12), and stability (disagreement reduced by 50--65\%), with \pninetyfive latency maintained within 1.15$\times$ of baseline.
This work establishes a practical foundation for training production-ready agents on commodity hardware.
